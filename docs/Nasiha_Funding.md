# Nasiha — Funding Strategy
*Draft — May 2026*

---

## Philosophy

Nasiha's core commitment is that knowledge exchange is free. Funding exists to sustain the Organization's operations — not to generate profit, not to influence what gets taught, and not to create dependency on any single source. A healthy funding model is diversified, transparent, and mission-aligned.

The Board of Directors shall maintain full transparency with the membership about how Nasiha is funded and how funds are used.

---

## Funding Sources

### 1. Grants

Grants are likely the most sustainable early funding path for a mission-driven non-profit in the medical education space. Nasiha should pursue grants from:

- **Health and medical education foundations** — organizations that fund knowledge access, professional development, and healthcare equity initiatives.
- **Government and public health bodies** — particularly those focused on global health, underserved communities, or medical workforce development.
- **International development organizations** — bodies like WHO affiliates or regional health agencies that fund capacity building in the medical field.

**What makes Nasiha competitive for grants:**
- A clear, documented mission with a measurable knowledge exchange model.
- International reach and accessibility to underserved communities.
- A volunteer-driven model that demonstrates efficient use of funds.
- Early data from the credit ledger showing real exchange activity.

**Actions:**
- [ ] Identify 10–15 grant opportunities aligned with Nasiha's mission in Year 1.
- [ ] Assign grant writing responsibility to a founding member or volunteer.
- [ ] Develop a standard grant application package (mission summary, impact metrics, financials).

---

### 2. Individual Donations

Members and supporters who believe in the mission should have an easy way to contribute financially. Nasiha should never pressure members to donate, but should make it simple and meaningful for those who want to.

**Donation model:**
- A voluntary annual contribution option for members who wish to support the Organization beyond their time contributions.
- A general public donation page for supporters who are not members but want to contribute to the mission.
- Named giving opportunities for significant donors — e.g., sponsoring a lecture series or a student membership cohort — with recognition in the community digest and annual report.

**What to avoid:**
- Tiered membership where donors get better access. Donations are separate from the credit system and confer no advantage in the knowledge exchange.
- Making members feel obligated. Donation asks should be infrequent and low-pressure.

---

### 3. Institutional Partnerships

Hospitals, medical schools, pharmaceutical companies, and healthcare organizations may wish to partner with Nasiha to support its mission. Institutional partnerships can provide both funding and access to new members.

**Partnership principles:**
- Partners may sponsor events, fund specific programs, or underwrite student memberships — but they do not influence content, curriculum, or community decisions.
- No advertising within the Nasiha platform or community spaces.
- Partnership agreements are reviewed and approved by the Board and disclosed to the membership.
- Pharmaceutical and commercial healthcare partnerships require additional scrutiny to ensure no conflict of interest with the Organization's educational mission.

**Partnership tiers (illustrative):**

| Tier | Contribution | Recognition |
|---|---|---|
| Community Partner | Small annual contribution | Listed in Nasiha digest and website |
| Supporting Partner | Moderate contribution | Named sponsorship of one event or program per year |
| Founding Partner | Significant contribution | Named in charter acknowledgments, annual report, and founding materials |

---

### 4. Events & Sponsorships

Nasiha's lecture series, webinars, and community events can attract external sponsors — organizations that want visibility with a professional medical audience without influencing content.

**Model:**
- Sponsors may underwrite the cost of hosting an event (platform fees, recording, promotion) in exchange for acknowledgment before or after the session.
- Sponsored events are clearly labeled as such. Sponsors have no input into content or speakers.
- Revenue from sponsored events flows into the Organization's general operating fund.
- Nasiha should avoid becoming overly reliant on event sponsorship — it can create pressure to prioritize popular topics over mission-critical ones.

---

## Fee Policy

Whether Nasiha should ever charge members for anything is a question the Board of Directors will determine as the Organization matures. The guiding constraint is clear: **knowledge exchange within Nasiha is always free.** Any fees, if introduced, would apply only to specific operational costs (such as certifications, printed materials, or optional services) and never to the core mission of learning and teaching.

This policy will be reviewed annually by the Board.

---

## Financial Governance

- The Board of Directors approves an annual budget before the start of each fiscal year.
- The Treasurer maintains accurate financial records and reports to the Board quarterly.
- An annual financial summary is shared with the full membership for transparency.
- All funding sources and amounts are disclosed in the annual report.
- No single funding source should represent more than 40% of annual revenue — to protect the Organization's independence.

---

## Early Phase Funding Reality

In the founding cohort phase, Nasiha will operate on minimal resources — primarily the time and goodwill of its founding members. Before pursuing grants or partnerships, the Organization needs:

- [ ] Non-profit registration and legal status established
- [ ] A bank account and basic financial infrastructure
- [ ] A compelling one-page funding pitch summarizing the mission, model, and early traction
- [ ] At least a few months of operating history and exchange activity to demonstrate proof of concept

The founding members should expect to self-fund early operational costs (domain registration, basic tools, communications) before external funding is in place.
